Doppler Effect / Doppler Shift

In class, teachers usually give an explanation of the Doppler's Effect by letting students imagine an ambulance 🚑 (source of sound) passing by an observer 🧍‍♂️ and it goes like 🚨eeeEEEEeeooo..🚨 or so they said.

Yet, having not heard of those before, it's probably hard to imagine, right?

This website allows you to simulate that experience using your hand✋ as a sound source and your device's camera📷 as a stationary observer, letting you explore how 🔊 frequency changes with distance and how it would be received 📥.

Let's see what the effect is all about:

What is the Doppler's Effect? 🌊
The Doppler Effect is the change in the observed frequency of a wave when there is relative motion between a wave source and an observer🔭.

☑️ When the source of a wave moves towards the observer, the waves become compressed, causing the observed frequency to increase 📈 (higher pitch for sound).
☑️ When the source moves away from the observer, the waves become spread out, causing the observed frequency to decrease 📉 (lower pitch).

This effect occurs with all types of waves, including: Sound waves 🔊, Light waves💡, and Water waves💧. However, it is most easily observed with sound, such as the changing pitch of a passing ambulance siren.

Basic Relationship 🔊
For sound waves, the observed frequency (f') depends on:
👉 speed of the wave (v)
👉 velocity of the source ( vs)
👉 velocity of the observer ( v0)
A simplified relationship used is:

\[f_0=f_s\frac{v \pm v_o}{v \mp v_s}\]
